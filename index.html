Overview

The El Pueblo Community Resource Study is a community visioning document produced for the City of Tucson's Planning Department. It focused on three underserved sites on Tucson's south side: the El Pueblo Neighborhood Center, the Laos Transit Center, and the Tucson Rodeo Grounds. The south side has a history of fractured relationships with formal planning institutions, which made community trust a central challenge of the project.

Working under a formal scope of work over three months, the team conducted a site analysis, facilitated stakeholder interviews with 15 community members and city officials, and led public engagement events at the El Pueblo Senior Center and the Laos Transit Center. The final deliverable outlines current conditions, community visions, and implementation strategies across three themes: Healthy Neighborhoods, Sense of Place, and Accessibility.

Impact

Delivered as a formal planning document to the City of Tucson and recognized with the 2023 Planning Excellence Gold Award, the highest recognition in the University of Arizona's urban planning program. The document is now a reference resource for future planning of the El Pueblo Center area.

The 14 community stakeholders interviewed for the study, and the thematic venn diagram distilled from their feedback. Where three topic areas overlapped — transportation, environmental, economic, and historical — major themes emerged: Accessibility, Healthy Neighbourhoods, and Sense of Place.

Extreme Heat Vulnerability of Manufactured Housing

Graduate Research, University of Arizona / SW-IFL, January 2023–January 2024

Funder

U.S. Department of Energy, Office of Biological and Environmental Research

Role

Second author; GIS workflow, core dataset, first manuscript draft

Output

✓ Published: Urban Studies, 2025

Citation

Stoker, P., Santiago, X., & Kear, M. (2025). doi.org/10.1177/00420980251351241

View published paper View ArcGIS dashboard

Overview

This research looked at how vegetation cover varies by housing type and tenure arrangement, and what that means for residents' exposure to extreme heat. The study focused on mobile and manufactured housing communities in Phoenix-Mesa, Tucson, and Flagstaff, combining 1.7 million county tax assessor parcel records with satellite imagery classified at 1-meter resolution.

The full GIS workflow was designed and executed as part of this research role, covering image classification, building footprint integration, accuracy validation, and statistical analysis at the parcel level. The finalized dataset was compiled and released publicly to support reproducibility.

Key finding

Manufactured housing parcels have significantly less vegetation than single-family properties across all three cities, with MH units in park settings having the least of all. The vegetation gap between single-family and MH parcels ranges from 5% in Tucson to 9% in Phoenix, a measurable difference in heat exposure that comes down to how land is owned and managed, not just where it's located.

Study area map: Phoenix-Mesa (29.7°C avg), Tucson (28°C), and Flagstaff (17.1°C). Each city shown with urbanized footprint and 5-mile buffer zone.

Contributions

Executed the full GIS workflow: classified satellite imagery using a supervised machine learning algorithm, with manually drawn training samples for four land cover types across three metro areas
Identified and resolved a systematic classification error where dirt was being read as impervious surface; fixed by integrating building footprint data, which pushed overall accuracy above 90%
Ran zonal statistics to calculate vegetation cover per parcel and per building across 1.7 million properties
Built a proof of concept on a subset of the study area before scaling to the full dataset
Wrote the first full draft of the manuscript, covering the literature review, study area, data, and methods sections; contributed to two rounds of peer review revisions
Built an ArcGIS Online dashboard making parcel-level findings accessible to 50+ stakeholders across policy, public health, and planning

Project

Water Demand under Climate Change in the Salt River Project Service Area, Arizona

Partner

Salt River Project / University of Arizona

Output

Conference paper presented at CCWI/WDSA 2024, Ferrara, Italy

Period

2022–2024

Overview

The Salt River Project provides water to 13 cities in the Phoenix metro area. As temperatures rise and precipitation patterns shift, understanding how outdoor water demand will change under different climate scenarios is a critical planning question for the region. This project developed a quantitative framework to estimate those demand changes — accounting for pool evaporation, vegetation irrigation requirements, and historically observed irrigation inefficiencies across all 13 municipalities.

The GIS work centred on classifying land cover from NAIP multispectral imagery for 2010, 2015, and 2021, and using zonal statistics to calculate total vegetated and pool surface area by municipality. Those area estimates feed directly into the Penman-Monteith evapotranspiration equation used to project future outdoor demand under climate scenarios. The work was presented as a conference paper at the 2024 Computing and Control for the Water Industry / Water Distribution Systems Analysis joint conference in Ferrara, Italy.

What this produced

Land cover classifications for 13 Phoenix metro municipalities across three time periods (2010, 2015, 2021), with pool and vegetation surface area estimates by municipality. Results used to calculate outdoor irrigation inefficiency metrics and project water demand changes under future climate scenarios for SRP's long-range planning.

Vegetated land cover by municipality in the SRP service area (2010). Gilbert led at 28.3% vegetation; Phoenix had the largest total vegetated area at 43,519 acres. These figures fed directly into the Penman-Monteith outdoor demand calculations.

Contributions

Classified NAIP multispectral imagery (1m resolution, 4-band RGB-N) for 13 Phoenix metro cities across three time periods using a supervised maximum likelihood classifier with four land cover classes: vegetation, water surfaces, impervious, and bare ground
Manually delineated training sites for each class; assessed classification accuracy using stratified random sampling and ground-truthing against orthophoto images
Applied zonal statistics to calculate total pool and vegetation surface area per municipality for each time period — the key inputs for estimating evapotranspiration-based outdoor demand
Collaborated with the project team on methodology design and contributed to data curation and formal analysis; co-author on the conference paper

Overview

This assessment evaluated social vulnerability across all census block groups in Marina, CA, a coastal community in Monterey County facing compounding risks from sea level rise, coastal erosion, and climate-driven hazards. The analysis was conducted at Integral Consulting and the final report is a client deliverable; what's shown here is the working methodology.

The assessment built a 12-variable vulnerability index drawing on the CDC/ATSDR Social Vulnerability Index and EPA EJScreen demographic indicators, applied across 11 census block groups. Variables covered socioeconomic status, demographic composition, health burden, transportation access, and housing characteristics. Thresholds were set against city and county benchmarks to identify which block groups showed significant vulnerability across each dimension.

A vulnerability ranking across all Marina block groups, identifying two with 7 to 9 significant indicators out of 12 as highest priority. Findings fed directly into client recommendations for where to prioritize community resilience investment in a coastal hazard context.

Solar Energy Farm Site Suitability Analysis: Maricopa County, AZ

Context

University of Arizona Graduate Certificate in GIS

Tools

ArcMap, Raster Calculator, Euclidean Distance, Zonal Statistics

Period

Fall 2021

View full analysis

Overview

This analysis identifies suitable sites for utility-scale solar energy development in Maricopa County, Arizona, one of the highest solar irradiance regions in the country. The goal was to find parcels where land conditions, infrastructure access, and solar potential align well enough for development to be viable.

Seven weighted criteria were combined into a composite suitability surface, then cross-referenced against vacant, developable parcels to produce a shortlist of actionable sites. Two final candidate sites in Wittmann, AZ were selected for closer analysis, each covering 15 to 30 acres with over 50% highly suitable land and projected energy generation in the range of 800 to 1,400 MWh per month.

What this produced

A weighted composite suitability surface across Maricopa County, narrowed to 5 candidate parcels meeting size and suitability thresholds, with two sites analysed in detail including acreage, suitability proportion, and projected monthly energy generation.

Conference poster presenting the full site suitability analysis: seven weighted criteria maps, final composite suitability surface, and two candidate sites identified from vacant parcels meeting 50%+ high-suitability thresholds at 5+ acres.

Methods

Selected 7 suitability criteria through a literature review of comparable solar suitability studies, weighting global horizontal irradiance most heavily (30%) followed by distance to electric substations (20%) and slope (15%)
Processed and reclassified raster datasets for aspect, elevation, slope, land cover, annual temperature, and global horizontal irradiance, all extracted to the Maricopa County boundary
Converted substation vector data to raster using the Euclidean Distance tool, then reclassified into suitability tiers for use in the weighted overlay
Generated the final weighted suitability surface using the Raster Calculator, combining all seven criteria into a single composite score
Used Zonal Statistics to cross-reference high-suitability areas with vacant developable parcels, filtering by minimum size (5+ acres) and suitability proportion (50%+) to produce the final candidate list

Grocery Store Site Suitability Analysis: Baltimore City, MD

Context

University of Arizona Graduate Certificate in GIS

Tools

ArcGIS Pro, Baltimore City Open Data

Period

Fall 2021

View full analysis

Overview

Food insecurity in Baltimore City was already a serious structural problem before COVID-19. Nearly 100,000 residents were food insecure, with a $59 million annual food budget shortfall. During the pandemic, household food insecurity in Maryland spiked from 11% to 22%, compounding long-standing inequities tied to disinvestment, poverty, and racially segregated neighbourhood conditions.

This analysis uses four equity-driven criteria to identify which Baltimore community statistical areas are most in need of new grocery store development, using the Baltimore City Health Department's Healthy Baltimore 2020 objectives as a benchmark. Site selection went a step further by identifying city-owned vacant properties within the highest-need areas as concrete, immediately actionable development opportunities.

What this produced

A scored suitability map across all Baltimore community statistical areas, plus a set of city-owned vacant properties identified as candidate grocery store sites, available for purchase via open bid within the highest-need neighbourhoods.

Final suitability scores across Baltimore community statistical areas (0–3 scale). Darkest areas met three of four equity criteria; no area met all four, reflecting the complexity of overlapping need.

Methods

Designed an equity-centred criteria framework using four variables: percent Black/African American residents (threshold: 61.8%), percent households below the poverty line (threshold: 21.2%), fast food outlet density per 1,000 persons (threshold: 0.88), and distance from existing grocery stores (threshold: more than 0.5 miles)
Thresholds were anchored to city-wide averages and Baltimore City Health Department objectives rather than arbitrary cutoffs, grounding the analysis in local equity benchmarks
Scored all community statistical areas on a 0–4 scale based on how many criteria thresholds they met; no area scored a perfect 4, reflecting the complexity of overlapping need
Overlaid highest-scoring areas with city-owned vacant building data to surface properties available for redevelopment as grocery store locations
All data sourced from Baltimore City Open Data portal; analysis conducted in ArcGIS Pro

Project

Using UAS to Measure Land Surface Temperature and Classify Land Cover: Tucson, AZ

Context

RNR 522, University of Arizona Graduate Certificate in GIS

Tools

DJI Mavic 2 Pro, Pix4D Mapper, ArcGIS Pro

Period

Fall 2023

View full presentation

Overview

This project explored whether land surface temperature varies meaningfully across different types of land cover and slope orientation, a question with direct relevance to urban heat and climate resilience work. The study area was a 15.6-acre private property in Tucson containing a mix of vegetation, impervious surfaces, bare ground, and three small water bodies, which made it a useful test case for understanding how cover type affects thermal conditions at a fine scale.

The workflow ran from field data collection through photogrammetric processing, thermal conversion, and spatial analysis. A drone collected 160 thermal and RGB images in a single flight at 328 feet above ground level. The resulting land cover classification achieved 85% overall accuracy, and temperature analysis confirmed that bare ground runs hottest (19.2°C average) while water stays coolest (12.5°C). East-facing slopes recorded the highest average temperature at 22.2°C.

What this produced

A complete drone-to-map pipeline: orthomosaic, surface and terrain models, canopy height model, land cover classification (85% accuracy), and a land surface temperature layer broken down by cover type and slope direction. Very few planning students have direct experience collecting and processing drone-based thermal remote sensing data.

RGB orthomosaic generated from 160 drone images showing the 15.6-acre study area.

Methods

Planned and flew a single-grid drone mission at 328ft above ground level with 75% forward and 80% side overlap, collecting 160 thermal and RGB images on November 8, 2023
Processed all imagery in Pix4D Mapper to generate a 3D point cloud, digital surface model, digital terrain model, and RGB orthomosaic
Converted raw thermal imagery to land surface temperature values using a MATLAB script, producing a continuous temperature raster across the study area
In ArcGIS Pro, derived slope and aspect models from the terrain model, built a canopy height model by subtracting the terrain from the surface model, and calculated the Green Leaf Index to separate living vegetation from soil and other surfaces
Ran a supervised land cover classification using training samples, reaching 85% overall accuracy across four classes: vegetation, impervious, bare ground, and water
Calculated mean land surface temperature by cover class and slope direction, finding bare ground hottest (19.2°C), followed by impervious (17.4°C), vegetation (16.2°C), and water (12.5°C). East-facing slopes were warmer than any other direction at 22.2°C

Downtown Chicago: Accessibility Matrix, Cartographic Design & Spatial Communication

Context

LAR 570 / PLG 597, University of Arizona

Tools

ArcGIS Pro, EPA Smart Location Database, RTAMS transit data

Period

Overview

This coursework focused on cartographic design as a discipline in its own right. The primary piece is a multi-map series on food access and transportation equity in downtown Chicago, built around the question of how to present related spatial datasets so each map holds up on its own while together they tell a coherent story.

The second piece is a schematic diagram of the CTA Red Line, a standalone exercise in transit map design. The accessibility matrix series is about layering multiple variables across space. The Red Line diagram takes the opposite approach, stripping everything back to communicate a single network's structure as clearly as possible.

The design challenge

Good cartographic design means the reader understands the data without being told how to read it. For the accessibility matrix, that meant applying consistent visual language across all maps: colour palette, typography, scale, and layout, so a viewer moving between panels immediately sees the spatial relationships. For the Red Line diagram, it meant deciding what to leave out.

Composite accessibility matrix showing walkability index, grocery store status, and zero-car households across downtown Chicago census block groups.

Accessibility Matrix: map series

Designed four individual maps (walkability index, grocery store locations, zero-car households, and a composite accessibility matrix), each working as a standalone while building toward the composite view
Applied rule of thirds for figure-to-text ratio and grid-based column alignment throughout, so the layout stays consistent across all panels
Sourced walkability and zero-car household data from the EPA Smart Location Database at census block group level; grocery store locations from City of Chicago open data
The subject matter was chosen deliberately: where low walkability, poor food access, and high zero-car household rates overlap, residents face real compounded barriers to getting healthy food

CTA Red Line: schematic diagram

Designed as a standalone diagrammatic transit map, prioritizing legibility over geographic accuracy
Station names, transfer points, and line routing were styled to communicate the network's structure at a glance, in the tradition of schematic transit cartography

Chicago Environmental Action Plan

Graduate Studio, University of Arizona, 2023

Course

University of Arizona Master of Urban Planning

Scale

City of Chicago: energy, air & climate, water, biodiversity, agriculture, waste

Output

25-page environmental action plan with city profile, equity goals, and sector strategies

Overview

This environmental action plan sets out a long-term sustainability framework for the City of Chicago, structured around six interconnected focus areas: energy, air and climate, water, biodiversity, agriculture, and waste management. The plan opens with a full city profile covering demographic data, housing tenure, transportation patterns, and baseline environmental conditions, then moves into sector-specific goals, strategies, and policy actions for each area.

The plan's central equity argument runs throughout: environmental burdens in Chicago fall disproportionately on Black, Latino, and low-income communities, and climate resilience efforts need to be designed to close those gaps. Each sector section follows a consistent goal-strategy-action structure, drawing on Chicago's existing climate plans, energy benchmarking data, and the "We Will Chicago" comprehensive plan to anchor recommendations in real policy context.

Equity thread

The plan's 20-year vision centres environmental justice across all six sectors, from clean energy tax districts in underserved neighbourhoods to urban agriculture incentive zones in food desert areas. Toronto's Eco-Roof Incentive Program is referenced as a model for green roof policy, a sign that Canadian cities are already doing some of this work.

Air and climate profile — GHG emissions by sector, 30-year climate normals, and air quality benchmarks used to set the plan's reduction targets.

What the plan covers

City profile with demographic, economic, housing, and transportation data drawn from 2020 ACS 5-year estimates and Chicago municipal reports
Energy: goals to reach 50% renewable energy in 10 years, with strategies including mandatory microgrids for large residential buildings and Clean Energy Tax Districts for low-income areas
Air and climate: goals to reduce particulate matter and ozone-forming emissions by 50%, backed by strengthened industrial emissions ordinances and a 250,000-tree urban canopy initiative
Water: goals addressing stormwater quality and affordability for low-income households, with strategies including green roof requirements for commercial development and a residential rainwater harvesting rebate program
Biodiversity: goals for new protected areas and native plantings, supported by a Chicago Biodiversity Monitoring Program building on Lincoln Park Zoo wildlife research
Agriculture: goals targeting food desert conditions and urban farming barriers, with strategies including revising the Urban Agriculture Zoning Ordinance and establishing Urban Agriculture Incentive Zones modelled on California programs
Waste management: goals for a 50% increase in residential recycling and 75% diversion of demolition materials from landfills, with a material exchange incentive program and incinerator site analysis framework

Rocinha: Critical Issues in Favela Urbanism

Seminar Report, University of Arizona CAPLA, International Planning, 2023

Course

PLG696D: International Planning, Dr. Seth Okyere

Role

Sole author

Location

Rocinha, Rio de Janeiro, Brazil — population 150,000–200,000

View full report

Overview

This report analyzed the critical urbanism challenges facing Rocinha, an informal settlement of up to 200,000 residents on the hillside of Dois Irmãos mountain in Rio de Janeiro's southern zone. It sits between two of the city's most affluent neighborhoods, a geographic reality that shapes both its political visibility and the nature of the government interventions it has received.

The report covered five interconnected issues: extreme construction density, tuberculosis rates eleven times Brazil's national average, natural hazard vulnerability, waste and wastewater management failure, and poor integration with the formal city. It evaluated the Favela-Bairro program (1995–2008) and the PAC Growth Acceleration Program (2007–2014).

What made this hard

The PAC program spent R$160 million in Rocinha and produced a celebrated footbridge while largely bypassing basic sanitation. Evaluating why well-funded interventions fall short requires separating political optics from resident need. Understanding how pressure to perform for international audiences ahead of the 2014 World Cup and 2016 Olympics shaped what got built and what got left out was central to that analysis.

Rocinha, Rio de Janeiro. Brazil's largest favela, home to an estimated 150,000–200,000 residents on the hillside of Dois Irmãos mountain.

What the report covered

Detailed analysis of five intersecting infrastructure and public health challenges, with supporting data and spatial context
Critical review of the Favela-Bairro program, including a 97% increase in housing prices within upgraded favelas between 1995 and 1997 and the displacement that followed
Analysis of the PAC program's gap between stated ambitions and actual delivery, looking at the role of the World Cup and Olympics and how residents responded
Recommendation for MIT's LiDAR mapping initiative as a foundation for genuine participatory planning in informal settlements
Engagement with Southern urbanism theory, informal settlement governance, and urban metabolism frameworks

Barcelona Superblocks: Graduate City Report

Academic Report, University of Arizona CAPLA, Transportation & Land Use Planning, 2023

Course

PLG568: Transportation and Land Use Planning

Role

Sole author

Focus

Pedestrianization, traffic calming, public space activation, health outcomes

View full presentation

Overview

This report examined Barcelona's Superblocks program, a city-scale urban mobility intervention that clusters nine city blocks into car-free interior zones, reroutes through-traffic to perimeter roads, and reclaims interior streets for pedestrian use and community activity.

The report analyzed the design logic and implementation of the Eixample Superblock, measured outcomes across air quality, noise, and active transportation, and drew out the planning principles that could apply elsewhere. It also engaged critically with equity concerns around the program, particularly gentrification pressure and who ends up benefiting from public space improvements.

Planning context

Car-centric street design is one of the most persistent barriers to healthy, livable cities. The Superblocks model shows how cities can reclaim street space in stages, with documented reductions in NO2 concentrations, road traffic noise, and urban heat, without requiring wholesale infrastructure reconstruction.

What the report covered

Design logic of the Superblock model: how traffic is rerouted, how streets are redesigned, and how public space gets programmed once cars are removed
Measured outcomes including air quality improvements, reduced noise exposure, and increased active transportation uptake
Public health benefits tied to more walkable streets, including reduced stress and more opportunities for physical activity
Equity and displacement concerns raised by residents and researchers, and how the program has responded to them
Transferability to North American cities: what conditions need to be in place for this kind of intervention to work
